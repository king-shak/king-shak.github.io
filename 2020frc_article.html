2020 FRC Vision Targeting

Inspiration

This was done for the 2020 FRC game Infinite recharge, and part of the game involved shooting these yellow foam balls (called power cells) into the top of a tower to score points. We wanted to automate the process of lining up with and shooting power cells into the tower. Doing so would make us much efficient at scoring and thus much more competitive.

How it Works

It uses a Limelight to find the target and determine both how far away we are from it and our orientation in relation to it. A Limelight is an all-in-one computer vision platform that has a camera and a small computer on board that runs the computer vision pipelines. The pipelines are the bit of software that take the raw images from the camera and processes them to extract the information about the target.
Once we know how far away the target is, and our orientation in relation to it, we automatically line up with the target and configure the ball shooting mechanism such that the balls go straight through the goal in the tower. All of this is done in a fraction of a second, as opposed to spending multiple second manually lining up with it.

Challenges

The biggest challenges were the math behind all the calculations (to find our distance and orientation with respect to the target), and getting the Limelight's pipelines to properly pick up the target on the tower. This mainly took a lot of trial and error and experimenting to get it right. In the end, I believe it worked flawlessly the entire competition season (never failing even once).

What I Learned

Learning the physics behind the ball shooting mechanism was very interesting, as we got to apply
concepts such as projectile motion and the doppler effect.
Something I learned coding-wise was interpolation tables. Basically, we calibrated our ball shooting mechanism from 4 feet, 5 feet, 6 feet, etc. all the way to 24 feet, and our interpolation table would store the configuration of the mechanism at each distance. Basically, if the robot was 5.5 feet away from the tower, the interpolation table would take the entries from 5 feet and 6 feet and interpolate between them to get the best configuration for the ball shooter.
